# Interview Pitch

## 60-Second Pitch

I built this OSINT case investigation lab to show how I approach suspicious domains and questionable online identities. The cases are simulated, but I wrote them like real analyst notes: scope first, evidence collection, indicator correlation, findings, limitations, and final reporting.

The main thing I wanted to show is that I understand OSINT is not just collecting data. It is about explaining what the data means, what it does not prove, and what I would need next before making a stronger conclusion.

## Skills This Project Demonstrates

- passive OSINT workflow
- DNS record analysis
- WHOIS-style registration review
- email security checks with SPF, DMARC, and MX
- basic phishing-domain assessment
- fake company / fraud validation thinking
- IoC documentation
- report writing
- risk scoring with limitations
- awareness of false positives
- basic Python scripting with error handling

## Likely Recruiter Questions

### Why did you create this project?

I wanted a portfolio project that shows how I think during an investigation. A lot of beginner projects only list tools, but I wanted to practice documenting reasoning and confidence level.

### Are the cases real?

No. They are simulated on purpose. I used fictional domains and fictional outputs so the project stays safe, ethical, and easy to review.

### What makes this OSINT?

The workflow uses passive collection and analysis: DNS, WHOIS-style data, email records, public presence checks, and correlation of indicators. It does not rely on scanning or exploiting anything.

### What would you do differently in a real investigation?

I would preserve the original email, collect full headers, check internal logs if available, review passive DNS history, and use approved threat intelligence platforms. I would also follow the organization's rules of engagement.

### How do you avoid false positives?

I avoid treating one weak indicator as proof. For example, a missing DMARC record or a new domain can happen with legitimate businesses. I look for patterns and I write down limitations.

### What was the most useful thing you learned?

The useful part was learning to slow down before writing the conclusion. It is easy to say "this looks suspicious." It is harder, and more useful, to explain why and how confident I am.

## How This Relates to OSINT and Threat Intelligence Roles

This project connects to junior OSINT and CTI work because it practices the basics:

- identifying and documenting indicators
- checking domain and email infrastructure
- mapping phishing behavior to MITRE ATT&CK T1566
- writing reports that another person can review
- making recommendations without overstating the evidence

It also shows that I understand the difference between an observation and a conclusion. That is something I would expect to use every day in an analyst role.
